the Palace The image of the younger self running in a palace remains central because it reveals what was always there: innocence, home, and a version of the self not yet disciplined by pain. To become a father often means realizing that the child one failed to protect in oneself must no longer be abandoned. The inner child is not a weakness. It is evidence. Every large truth is tested in small rooms, small rituals, and daily conduct. The thread that runs through this book is simple: a man is changed more by becoming a father than by any title, victory, or public recognition. He may not understand it immediately, but his children move the axis of his life. If I wanted my children never to feel abandoned in the way I once did, I had to learn to stop abandoning the deepest truth in myself. That is where love stops being a word and becomes structure.
